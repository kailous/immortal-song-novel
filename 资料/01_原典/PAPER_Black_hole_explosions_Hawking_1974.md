# Black hole explosions? (1974)

**Author:** Stephen W. Hawking
**Journal:** Nature, Vol. 248, pp. 30–31
**Published:** March 1, 1974

## Abstract & Archival Summary

In this groundbreaking and paradigm-shifting paper, Stephen Hawking challenged the long-held classical view of general relativity that "nothing could escape a black hole." The paper outlines how quantum gravitational effects—typically disregarded in computations of black hole formation—could lead to significant macroscopic phenomena over extended periods.

Hawking mathematically demonstrated that black holes should emit particles (such as neutrinos or photons) at a rate consistent with the black hole having a specific temperature inversely proportional to its mass. This phenomenon is now universally known as **Hawking Radiation**. It implies that black holes are not entirely "black" but instead radiate energy and, consequently, steadily lose mass over time.

As a black hole loses mass, its surface gravity increases, further accelerating the emission rate in a runaway effect. Hawking postulated that very low-mass, or primordial, black holes would eventually evaporate and "explode". In its final 0.1 seconds, the micro-black hole would release an enormous amount of energy, equivalent to about a million 1-megaton hydrogen bombs. This discovery is celebrated for fundamentally bridging quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and general relativity.

### Significance for Hard Sci-Fi
This paper provides the ultimate mechanism for extracting energy from a singularity or explaining the explosive end of an artificially created micro-black hole (or a closing wormhole). In setting design, "Hawking Radiation" is the theoretical basis for detecting cloaked gravitational anomalies or weaponizing the evaporation of primordial black holes against an enemy fleet.
