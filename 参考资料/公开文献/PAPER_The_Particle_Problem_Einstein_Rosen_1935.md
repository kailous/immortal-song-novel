# The Particle Problem in the General Theory of Relativity (1935)

**Authors:** A. Einstein and N. Rosen
**Journal:** Physical Review, Vol. 48, pp. 73-77
**Published:** July 1, 1935 (Received May 8, 1935)

## Abstract & Archival Summary

This foundational paper investigates the possibility of an atomistic theory of matter and electricity within the framework of general relativity, aiming to exclude singularities of the field. Einstein and Rosen sought to achieve this by using only the variables of the general theory of relativity and the Maxwell theory. 

They proposed a modification to the gravitational equations to allow for regular solutions in static spherically symmetric cases. This led to a groundbreaking mathematical model where physical space is represented by two identical sheets connected by "bridges" (later known colloquially as **wormholes**, or specifically, the **Einstein-Rosen Bridge**). 

In this framework, these bridges represent particles. This approach provided a geometric interpretation of particles without singularities, suggesting that an elementary particle could be understood as a spatially finite bridge connecting these two sheets of space. The theory also naturally explained the absence of negative mass particles, as solutions corresponding to such masses would not permit the formation of a regular bridge. The authors noted that electrical charge and mass were found to be independent constants of integration.

### Significance for Hard Sci-Fi
This is the absolute bedrock paper for all "wormhole" space-travel concepts in science fiction. It mathematically proves that space can fold and connect two distant points via a bridge, provided singularities can be avoided. In the context of setting design, this paper provides the theoretical justification for "Instant Spacial Teleportation".
