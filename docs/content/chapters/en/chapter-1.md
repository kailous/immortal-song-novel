# Chapter 1: The Fallen Star

## I

The year 2056 CE. 39°N latitude, 116°E longitude.

What was once called Beijing had become a vitrified impact crater sunk deep into the earth's crust. Thirty years ago, an alien fleet's dimensional strike had punched clean through the bedrock of the North China Plain.

Twelve hundred meters below the surface. In the underground bunkers of the "Ark of Last Resort," the shattered turbines of the ventilation system ground out a piercing, dry shriek. The air was thick with rust, tar, and a despair that refused to leave.

"You're really going to send him?"

At the side of the control panel, an old physicist in a tattered hazmat suit clenched the master control lever with both hands, his voice so hoarse it was nearly broken: "I've done the math — I've run it three times! There is no time paradox. Changing the causes in the past cannot produce effects in the present! Even if he goes back and turns the tide, even if he gives humanity nuclear weapons a thousand years early to fire into space — for *our* timeline, nothing will change. The sky will still be black, and we will still die in this pit. We're spending the last reserves of all mankind to send him down a parallel branch that has nothing to do with us. What is the point? Do you know what's inside that corridor? It's a hell that grinds living people into molecular dust! Not a single living thing has ever come out of it in one piece — not one! And what we're about to send in there is a human being!"

The female officer slapped the old man's hand away. Her thin, bony fingers had gone blue at the joints. Sleepless nights had carved two hollow trenches beneath her eyes, yet the eyes themselves, fixed on the holographic display, remained cold and hard as iron.

"He is the only one with a chance. We don't know if there are any other timelines out there — but if there is even one where humanity can live with its head held high, we have to fight to open it."

Her finger stabbed at the red numbers pulsing at the edge of the screen. Her voice came out like sandpaper crushed underfoot:

"Forty-seven seconds. Tidal force peak, seventy-four thousand G-band. The stable window for the spatial corridor is forty-seven seconds."

She turned and looked toward the thirty-two-ton alloy blast door at the far end of the corridor.

Behind that door was a damaged alien engine — one humanity had spent one point two million lives and thirty years struggling to repair.

And humanity's one and only time traveler — Lin Mo.

## II

The airlock opened. A heavy smell of coolant drifted out.

The engine chamber was small. At its center floated an irregular black mass roughly three meters across. No power conduits, no mechanical gears. Its surface was covered in densely packed quantum-etched circuits, cycling a faint, cold light through the dead silence.

On the metal walkway leading to the engine core stood a man. A white form-fitting suit, thin as a cicada's wing, pressed flush against his skin, every contour visible through it. His muscles did not swell with the soft bulk of a bodybuilder's, but lay pressed flat against the bone in corded ridges, not a gram wasted — speaking to a soldier's particular efficiency.

Beneath the suit, his back bore no visible scars. Quantum-state reset had restored this body time and again to its most perfect "baseline." Yet in the deepest layer of bone and flesh, the memories of dum-dum rounds that had torn through him, of scalpels that had laid him open, remained — like concealed weld lines in steel, permanent and unseen.

On his left wrist, a seamless black metal band was clamped tight.

The Dimensional Bracer. The device obeyed no physical law of the three-dimensional universe. In the decade-plus since it was captured, humanity had tried nuclear-blast heat and tensile machines to destroy it — unable to leave so much as a micron-deep scratch. Inside it lay a higher-dimensional space — absolute zero temperature, no flow of time. This broken civilization had gathered every last thing it could and pressed it all into that space — machine tools, a miniature cold-fusion array, a backup of all human memory.

His name was Lin Mo.

A hundred years earlier, scrounging food from wild dogs on the Yellow River flats of the Central Plains, he had been called Sun Mancang.

None of that mattered now.

## III

"Corridor activates in one hundred and twenty seconds." From the speakers, the female officer's voice strained to hold steady.

The technicians withdrew from the room quickly. Not one looked back at him. No one dared to look at a man about to descend into hell.

The alien crew of that vessel had been carbon-silicon hybrids, their neural systems sustained by quantum entanglement. Even so, it had taken shifts of three to operate this engine, each lasting no more than fifteen seconds. Today, he would endure forty-seven seconds alone, in this carbon-based body of flesh and blood.

"Thirty seconds."

The black mass above began to vibrate violently. Light around him ceased to travel in straight lines; his shadow fractured into four displaced ghost-images. Real space was already beginning to shatter around him.

Lin Mo closed his eyes.

What rose in his mind was not the mission codes given him by the high command. It was 1940. A heavy snowfall in Henan.

Cracked palms bleeding at the joints, cupping two wild bird eggs dug from a frozen hollow in the dead grass. The shells were ice-cold, pressed hard against a belly that had known no grain for years — yet they sent a bitter, wild surge of joy through an empty gut. His mother tucked her chilblain-covered fingers into the front of her tattered jacket and, lips trembling gray-blue, murmured only: "Good, Mancang. Get through this winter, and we'll have made it."

Spring came the following year, and before its first day was out, the Japanese had razed the village.

"Ten seconds."

Lin Mo opened his eyes. A pair of entirely black pupils. No fear. No ripple. A hundred and sixteen years of slaughter and dying had scraped him clean of every feeling a living person ought to have.

The female officer did not look at any more flickering data. She spun sharply, facing the direction of the engine room, snapping her legs together.

Her left arm crossed below her collarbone, five fingers clenching her right upper arm with all she had; her right forearm rose straight up, right fist pressed hard against the hollow of her own neck. Muscles strained to their limit, not a tremor to spare.

This had once been the starship expeditionary force's last-resort posture in deep space when facing hull disintegration — a way to forcibly lock one's center of gravity. But through thirty years of apocalyptic slaughter, this broken posture had become the only military salute surviving humanity still passed down.

Not to gods. Not to commanders. Flesh locking against flesh, holding on with everything it had —

She raised her head, looking directly toward the engine room beyond the blast door, her voice fractured and clear:

"Humanity — endures!"

On the other side of the blast door, Lin Mo slowly raised his arm, left hand sealed against his right, right fist rising like a stake beside his shoulder. The two of them stood in silent communion across that door, each holding their own center of gravity in place.

Boom.

The emergency bulkhead panels lining the outer corridor were blown clean off. The gravitational wave pulse from the engine's ignition had been intercepted by the fleet outside — they had found this place.

"Three."

Alarms erupting.

"Two."

Footsteps thundering in the outer corridor. Countless. Closing in.

"Engine activated."

## IV

No light. Or rather, light was torn apart.

The bones of space lay exposed, like flayed blood vessels. In the first Planck instant of the corridor's opening, a gravitational differential of seventy-four thousand G shredded Lin Mo's body into molecular fragments.

No pain. His neural tissue had barely begun to register a signal before his cortex disintegrated along with it.

But the instinct inscribed in the deepest layer of his genes had activated. Consciousness was not required. As long as the cellular residue still clinging to the inside of the bracer was there, it would not stop.

Flesh grew madly from the gap. A network of nerves barely woven was immediately cut to bloody pulp by sideways tidal force. A few ribs barely formed were ground to ash by gravitational waves in the next instant.

Proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate; proliferate, obliterate, proliferate, obliterate, obliterate, proliferate, proliferate, obliterate proliferate obliterate obliterate proliferate proliferate obliterate obliterate proliferate obliproliferate terateoblit obliterproliferate ate obliterate……

Hundreds of millions of times per second. Each one a death.

Second forty-four.

The damaged corridor began to lose control. The missing navigation module collapsed in the storm. The temporal coordinate instrument, set to lock onto the 1970 radio station, went into wild overload.

1970 — missed.

1644 — missed.

The engine's energy exhausted itself in a millisecond. The corridor collapsed entirely.

An unremarkable black bracer, enclosing the last faint pulse of something barely larger than a sesame seed — a scrap of flesh and blood — was forced by the blast's shockwave out through the wound in spacetime, and fell, directly, into the endless black night of history.

## V

Southern Song Dynasty. The Shaoxing era.

North of Anji County, Huzhou. Deep in the outlying ranges of the Tianmu Mountains.

The twelfth lunar month, before dawn.

Heaven and earth were like an inverted icehouse of ten thousand years. Frozen mist pooled dense and unmoving in the mountain hollows, bound in a chill so bleak it pressed the chest. Withered grasses that had died early in the season were broken flat by congealed frost, crouching against the ground. Every beast and bird left breathing had long since burrowed to the deepest reaches of cold, lightless dens, and even their dream-cries were frozen to nothing by the winter's severity.

A strange, absolute stillness.

Without warning, a faint pop sounded in the air above — as light and hollow as a phantom string of bubbles bursting at the bottom of a glass vessel.

A thud.

A heavy, muffled impact — something had snapped several rotted branches clean off and embedded itself straight down into a heap of damp, cold dead leaves that had lain undisturbed for years.

The impact shattered the dead silence of an entire mountainside.

At the darkest center of the impact crater, beside the undamaged black ring, there clung the most pitiable thing — a ball of fresh blood no larger than a walnut.

Without heart or lung, without sinew or bone, it began to grow in the frozen earth of that remote place — both furiously manic and perfectly silent.

First, pallid calcium phosphate crystallized free in the dim moonlight, stacking joint by joint like the roots of an ancient gnarled tree into a complete spine; the ribs on both sides spread outward one by one, like a fan unfolding.

Then came muscle fibers, dark red as clay, spinning layer upon layer like silk from a spring silkworm — twisting, pulling, locking tight along the bones. Within the chest cavity, a shell of a heart slowly took shape; in the empty hollow, it suddenly struck its first beat, a muffled drum. When the five organs and six viscera had each found their place, a layer of skin fine as warm fresh wax quietly settled over the once-raw red flesh, wrapping this forge-hardened body of blood and bone into seamless completeness.

Two shi — roughly four hours — passed.

The sky had not yet broken into dawn, yet at the edge of the hollow there was unmistakably a person now.

By size and height, a frame far taller and broader than any common villager; by bearing, muscles knotted like cold iron throughout, lying bare upon the frost-covered earth — the skin strangely smooth and even, unmarked by the slightest dust.

Yet on the left wrist, still clamped fast, was the black iron ring — its cold gleam bone-chilling — still as a dead thing.

He began to breathe — shallow, barely there.

Yet above the chest, in the sea of his consciousness — a white expanse, scrubbed utterly clean. A thousand years of life and death, of a fate that licked at blades, had been washed down to not a single character remaining.

Only that crescent moon, pale as a newborn's skin, hung slanted at the western ridge — and fell, without favor, its fine, desolate frost-light in clusters all across his hair, spilled everywhere on the ground, black as ink.

Though a thin thread of pre-dawn light had finally worked through, the clouds at the Tianmu peak bore down on the treetops as though weighted with a thousand catties of lead. Only the cold mist that had refused to lift all night now grudgingly parted, just a little.

On the treacherous path strewn with loose rock, a village woman picked her way forward, each step uneven. This woman was Lu Xiaoxiao.

Nineteen years old — that was all. Had she remained in her grandfather's old merchant house in Lin'an, she would have been dressed in gold-threaded robes, powdering her face. But this was a ruined world: what she wore now was a coarse hemp shift of indeterminate original color, patched over and over in rough, uneven layers, and outside it a ragged sheepskin half-jacket that had long since shed its fur — one gust of mountain wind and a reek of rancid oil and rot drifted from it all around her. Her hair, which had once been a lustrous black, was now free of any oil or dressing, pinned up haphazardly with a rough wooden hairpin, which only made the skin and bone exposed to the wind look more pallid — the colorless pallor of frost and pressure, utterly without life.

Yet on one thin, fragile shoulder she carried hard a worn mulberry-wood shoulder pole, polished to a patina by years of use. From its front end dangled a bundle of dead branches and firewood; in the basket at the back, a few wild mountain chicken eggs lay alone at the bottom, their skins shriveled, their cold stiff, and their surfaces faintly astringent.

In the village, the kind-hearted sighed in private: "Poor widow of the Zhang family." The hard-hearted spat behind her back: "A White Tiger Star, that one — brings death wherever she goes."

It was precisely to avoid those shallow-eyed, coarse-faced lowlifes that she had today gone the extra effort to push half a li deeper into this uninhabited mountain gully, hoping only to gather a few more branches of wild jujube wood that nobody else would want.

As she walked, the tattered rope-soled sandal on her foot suddenly caught.

Her nostrils went rigid in an instant — blood.

She had lived in the mountains long enough to know the difference: this was not the rank stench of a wild sow in heat, nor the ferret-stink of a weasel's leftover prey. This smell of blood carried the particular quality that came only when a living thing was stripped of its life in an instant — thick, hot, raw, and throat-searing.

Xiaoxiao's already-furrowed brows locked into a hard knot. She gripped the slick worn mulberry pole until the veins on both hands stood out; she swallowed even her breath back down, peered through the corner of her eye to gauge the situation, and along the lee side of a dead tree — half her body cautious, every nerve at maximum alert — she edged a half-step forward.

What met her eyes was a pool of warm, cloudy blood — enough to saturate and dissolve months of layered dead leaves and rotted branches packed into the earth below.

And at the very center of the blood pool, lying sprawled and open on his back, was a man.

A powerfully built man, bare from head to toe, without a stitch to cover him.

Xiaoxiao's soul left her body halfway on the spot; she felt a cold sweat rise at the root of every hair at her temples. These past years alone and without support, she had come across a few rigid-dead vagrants and fallen households in these famine-stricken mountain forests — but she had never seen anything like this manner of death.

The blood-soaked mud on the ground could have seeped through two inches of earth — yet this terrifying man, from head to toe, on every inch of that cold-hard steel-cast body, was clean and smooth as a shelled water chestnut. Not a wound the size of a sesame seed to be found anywhere.

Xiaoxiao ground her teeth and swallowed the shaking, and in the end gathered enough nerve on her own to cross to him, feet unsteady. She pressed two fingers together, held her wrist suspended, and reached toward the tendon on one side of his neck.

The flesh still held a living person's faint, warm give — and beneath her fingers, a sudden lurch.

Not yet dead.

The nineteen-year-old Widow Zhang sat stiff beside the murky red pool of blood-mud, her eyes fixing on this man's utterly motionless body. All the thousand calculations circling in her heart came down to one thing: the Kitchen God Offering Night, two days away. The broken-down Wang San and several other lowlifes from the village had long since battered down her courtyard gate, leaving word that on the night when the ancestral hall stood empty they would commit their beastly act. She was alone. Even if she fought those dregs tooth and nail at midnight with her wood-chopping blade, the outcome was still a body broken and swallowed alive.

At the thought, a bleakly desperate light flickered through Xiaoxiao's eyes. So be it. Alive is death; dead is death — she might as well call this a medicine sent by King Yama to save her life.

She stripped the ragged sheepskin jacket from her own body — it still held a few degrees of a woman's warmth — gripped it in her hands, and edged closer to the blood pool. She cast one glance downward, then looked away at once. This is just saving a life, she told herself, the rough sheepskin twisting tighter in her palm. Living is a person, dead is a corpse. Don't think too hard.

She looked again.

Then snapped her eyes shut. Working entirely by feel, she maneuvered the half-jacket toward his waist and hips. Her wrist slipped into empty air, and her fingertips grazed something cold-hard as cast iron yet threaded through with an unsettling warmth. She yanked her hand back as if burned. Xiaoxiao clenched her back teeth, shut her eyes, fingers trembling uncontrollably, and through the oily sheepskin, blindly worked that troublesome stretch of him into some semblance of order and covered it properly.

The jacket was finally on. She sank down beside the murky red mud, her pulse hammering so loud it filled both ears, heart taking a long while to settle. Nothing to it, she told herself, swallowing mouthfuls of ice-cold mountain air that tasted of blood. He's just a person. What's the matter with that? But then she found, with a bleak dismay, that her fingertips still carried the exact grain of that skin — its cold smoothness, its faint roughness — and the weight of the man's slow, faint breathing had already been pressed, without her permission, into the center of her palm, in the dark.

The mountain chicken eggs in the basket — left behind. The mulberry pole she kept for self-defense — abandoned.

She bent her back low, locked her grip onto his great arms, thick as boulders, and hauled this dead weight — the worth of two or three ordinary people — entirely onto her own slender back, using every last scrap of stubborn strength she had.

Her sandals — already coming apart at the seams, held together by a few scraps of twisted grass cord — now staggering under this mountain-weight, stumbled three steps for every fall and swayed every fifth across the frost-edged, blade-cold slick rocks. Frozen mud-spikes, sharp as knives, worked their way relentlessly through every crack in Xiaoxiao's much-mended foot-wrappings.

Through all of this — the burning exertion, the crushing weight — the man's broad, cold jaw knocked obliviously, again and again, against the point of her protruding shoulder blade. With each thread-thin breath still left in him, one pale warm cloud after another puffed straight against the back of her neck, already soaked through with cold sweat.

And on his left wrist, that lump of black iron — cold enough to chill to the marrow, still as a bottomless abyss — rode the jolting of the mountain road in the bone-cutting north wind —

One knock.

Then another.

Heavy and dull, without a trace of mercy, driving deep into the small of her back — thin as a sheet of paper, yet somehow still holding.

**(End of Chapter 1)**
