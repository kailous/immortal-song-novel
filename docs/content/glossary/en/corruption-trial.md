# Corruption Crisis and the Trial of Blood
> Legal Iron Law · The Final Price of a Godlike Regime

## Core Contradiction: Dragon-Slayers Become Dragons
As the Song Federation grows from regional industrialization into planetary rule, the once-pure founding coalition—craftsmen, generals, and the protagonist’s descendants—inevitably decays under absolute power and material temptation.

- **Termites**: Officials who exploit monopoly control over technical blueprints for rent-seeking and siphon funds from civil development.
- **The fall of the divine bloodline**: Some of the protagonist’s descendants, especially those with unusual traits, begin to view commoners as expendable and collude with remnants of the old order, running private courts and killing at will.

## The Great Trial
Once discovered, the protagonist refuses private punishment. Instead, he chooses the most extreme method possible: **a global public tribunal**.

The principal defendant is his most trusted eldest son—or another key descendant with strategic value. Accomplices include decorated founders and compromised academy officials.

**Execution protocol**: No extra-legal mercy is permitted. Before a world audience, the protagonist signs the death order and carries out the sentence in public before the Lin’an obelisk.

This marks the point at which Song law rises above kinship and bloodline, publicly destroying the old consensus that nobles stand above punishment and family must shield family.

## Secondary Damage: The Collapse of Family Warmth
**The protagonist’s final loneliness**: After the trial, he loses the last remaining sense of “home” in the human world and becomes only a sacred executioner of civilization, separated from the living by a gulf filled with his own family’s dead.

**The mother’s estrangement**: Intellectually she understands why a corrupt child had to die. Emotionally she cannot accept that the father was the executioner. After the trial she withdraws into silence, physically present but spiritually distant. That fracture becomes one of the heaviest emotional prices he ever pays.

## Institutional Remedy: Royal Power Isolation
The protagonist promulgates an **Imperial Descendants Act**: princes may still pursue honor, military distinction, and research achievement, but they may never hold real administrative office or command budgetary authority.

**Underlying logic**: Because the public treats the protagonist as quasi-divine, any prince becomes an inevitable political access point for opportunists. Cutting off their path to power is the only way to shield them from corruption.
